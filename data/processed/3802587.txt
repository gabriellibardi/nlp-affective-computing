Cognition-driven Adaptive Semantic Decoding Framework for Multimodal Sentiment Analysis JIAJIA TANG, GANG XIE, HONGGANG LIU, XUANYU JIN, JIANHAI ZHANG, and W ANZENG KONG, Key Laboratory of Brain Machine Collaborative Intelligence of Zhejiang Province, College of Computer Science, Hangzhou Dianzi University, Hangzhou, China In real-world scenarios, multimodal sentiment analysis faces significant challenges, particularly in crossscenario generalization. Existing works fail to effectively deal with the variability in evaluation frameworks and modality combinations, which results in poor transfer performance across different application contexts. In this article, the cognition-driven adaptive semantic decoding framework (CASDF) is proposed to realize an evaluation system and modality-independent multimodal sentiment analysis. Specifically, the adaptive modality association module is proposed to construct the adaptive modality mapping space, which allows us to dynamically adapt to arbitrary modality combinations. This indeed breaks through the limitation of the modality number and effectively deals with the modality gap. Furthermore, similar to the human hierarchical cognition (“perception-concept-decision”), the evaluation system progressive alignment module is presented to establish the unified evaluation system. This consists of the perception, concept, and decision analysis, which contributes to the adaptive cross-task analysis from the discrete sentiment space to the continuous sentiment space. The above joint analysis of the evaluation system and modality number indeed leads to the more flexible and generable multimodal sentiment semantic decoding paradigm. The experiments demonstrate that our sentiment semantic analysis network can achieve state-of-the-art performance. Tang et al. 1 Introduction Affective computing, which served as the core enabling technology for human–computer interaction and mental health monitoring, has made significant progress in clinical applications such as depression screening [15, 58] and autism intervention [29]. The above advancements are largely driven by the multimodal perception paradigm that integrates audio, text, visual, and physiological signals into the joint representations. However, current works are generally constrained by fixed modality combinations and predefined evaluation frameworks (e.g., six-category discrete emotion labels). This indeed limits the ability to generalize across different scenarios (e.g., from clinical diagnosis to social media analysis) and adapt to diverse tasks (e.g., from discrete emotion classification to continuous dimensional prediction). Intuitively, existing works fail to effectively deal with the misalignment issue between the cognitive paradigms and analysis architecture, and the misalignment issue between the modality combination and analysis architecture. Note that, different application scenarios adopt heterogeneous cognitive frameworks. For the discrete emotion classification tasks, the works [28, 30] attend to the sentiment analysis of 7 basic emotion categories. Similarly, the works [9, 40] focus on the more fine-grained sentiment analysis task that consists of 9 or 26 emotion categories. Moreover, for the continuous dimension-based emotion classification tasks, the work [1] relies on the specific emotion dimension, e.g., the valence and arousal dimension. The above discrete or specific dimension-based emotion analysis fails to decode the sentiments associated with the subtle discrepancy, e.g., the sadness sentiment and melancholy sentiment. This indeed makes it difficult for sentiment analysis architecture to establish a transferable cross-scenario semantic representation space. Moreover, for the modality analysis, different modalities exhibit significant heterogeneity. For instance, the visual modality may rely on facial micro-expressions [30] or body language [40]. The audio modality may emphasize the extraction of prosodic features [3] or speech content [4]. The textual modality attends to account for both semantic decoding [12] and pragmatic analysis [5]. That is, different modalities focus on investigating the modality-aware properties. Existing models predominantly use a static multimodal fusion paradigm during the modality fusion procedure [47], e.g., the modality number is fixed. This may fail to investigate the cross-modality inter-correlations when the modality is absent, or the new modality is transmitted to the model. The above two challenges naturally result in significant cross-scenario generalization issues. The sentiment analysis performance degradation arises from a dual coupling effect (see Figure1). As for the cognitive framework, variations in evaluation frameworks across scenarios cause feature space misalignment. At the modality interaction level, dynamic changes in modality combinations necessitate the re-learning of cross-modal association patterns. More importantly, current stateof-the-art models predominantly rely on static fusion architectures, which inherently lack the adaptability needed to accommodate variations in modality composition. The results in failing to address the combinatorial explosion problem that arises in complex and real-word environments. In conclusion, the above challenges highlight the fundamental contradiction in the multimodal emotion analysis task, i.e., the traditional modeling paradigm based on closed-world assumptions is deeply misaligned with the dynamics of open environments and the continuity of emotion recognition tasks. Overcoming the aforementioned bottleneck requires the development of a novel multimodel sentiment decoding paradigm centered around three essential aspects: (1) constructing a cross-task affective semantic ontology to achieve topological mapping of different evaluation systems; (2) developing decoupled modality representation learning block to facilitate flexible and adaptive reasoning over varying modality combination; and (3) designing a hierarchical alignment mechanism inspired by cognitive mechanism to simulate the progressive processing of human affective cognition. The research demonstrates that human brains process information through hierarchical semantic integration, i.e., the inputs are transmitted to abstract concepts in a progressive way [16, 21, 54]. Specifically, the above process consists of feature association, relational abstraction, and behavioral adaptation. Inspired by the above cognitive mechanism, we attend to the following analysis: (1) primary sensory feature integration in early fusion layers, (2) cross-modal concept formation via hippocampal-inspired memory binding, and (3) prefrontal-like decision calibration to support task-specific adaptation. This bio-inspired processing cascade directly informs our architecture design, particularly in simulating (1) feature integration in early fusion layers, (2) cross-modal concept construction inspired by the hippocampal memory binding mechanism, and (3) task adaptation inspired by the prefrontal decision calibration mechanism. During the feature matching period, the learnable projection module is leveraged to implement cross-modal signal alignment [2]. This approach establishes a cross-scenario unified semantic space that simulates human affective cognition, progressing from low-level feature perception to high-level abstract decision-making. Human emotional understanding follows a continuous progression-“signal perception → concept formation → value judgment”-rather than operating through discrete classification. This indeed breakthrough the inherent limitations of traditional multimodal fusion techniques. The above procedures naturally contribute to zero-shot generalization across varied modality configurations rather than the fixed and predefined modality combination. Moreover, the proposed framework adapts to arbitrary modality combinations, the case of a modality missing.
